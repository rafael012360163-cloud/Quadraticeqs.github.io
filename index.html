What are quadratic equations?

In mathematics, a quadratic equation (from Latin quadratus 'square') is an equation that can be rearranged in standard form as \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) ,
where the variable x represents an unknown number, and a, b, and c represent known numbers.
Because the quadratic equation involves only one unknown, it is called "univariate". The quadratic equation contains only powers of x that are
non-negative integers, and therefore it is a polynomial equation. In particular, it is a second-degree polynomial equation, since the greatest power is two.

Quadratic equations were solved with geometric and arithmetic methods by ancient civilizations long before modern algebra. The Babylonians (c. 2000 BCE) used completing the square to solve the equations, and the Egyptians (c. 2000-1650 BC) also explored them.

There are three ways to solve quadratic equations, which are factorization, completing the square, and the quadratic formula.
